Key Mechanistic Insights

1. Phe50 is the allele discrimination switch.
Removing Phe50 (F51A) swings the delta from −0.067 to +0.311 — a +0.378 shift. The aromatic ring makes van der Waals contacts with K392's aliphatic chain at 3.84Å. Without it, K392's longer side chain has no advantage.
2. N392 selectivity and ERAP1 selectivity are anti-correlated.
Every N392-favoring mutation also increases ERAP1 binding, because ERAP1 has Asn (N375) at the equivalent position. The binder contacts residue 392 directly — it cannot distinguish ERAP2-N392 from ERAP1-N375.
3. Electrostatic repulsion hypothesis disproven.
F51K and F51R were predicted to repel K392 (+/+ repulsion), but instead they prefer K392 (delta −0.225 and −0.157). Long flexible side chains find favorable hydrophobic packing with K392's aliphatic chain.
4. Negative control confirmed mechanism.
N38D (Asp−) shows delta = −0.223, confirming that negative charge at position 37 attracts K392's positive Lys — the electrostatic model works at the H-bond position.
5. Double mutations cancel rather than combine.
All 4 double mutants performed worse than expected. Combining two interface mutations destabilizes the binding mode cooperatively, causing Boltz-2 re-docking artifacts.

Why This Can't Work (Structural)

The V2 binder contacts position 392 through exactly 2 residues: Asn37 (H-bond at 4.36Å) and Phe50 (vdW at 3.84Å). The adjacent ERAP2-unique divergent residues (Y398, A403, D414) are all >6Å from any binder residue — the binder doesn't contact them.

To prefer N392 over K392, a mutation must better accommodate Asparagine at position 392. But ERAP1 has N375 (also Asparagine) at the structurally equivalent position. There is no way to distinguish ERAP2-N392 from ERAP1-N375 when the contact residue itself is identical.
